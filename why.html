Why Daily Benji

A morning paper for your money.

Why we built it — and who it's for.

Daily Benji started as something we built for ourselves. We didn't want a budgeting app — we'd tried them. They wanted our bank logins, or a zero-based budget, or a standing weekly date with a dashboard we'd stop opening by the third week.

We gave the popular ones a real shot, and they kept getting in the way of the one thing we wanted. There were upsells and credit-card offers, and to see anything useful we had to hand over the keys to our accounts. All we actually wanted was to know. Not to optimize, not to be coached or scolded — just to see where the money went, calmly, without turning it into a project.

So we built the thing we wanted. Forward your bank alerts to a private inbox, and every morning you get one tidy summary of yesterday. We named it after the morning paper — the thing that used to land on the doorstep, tell you what happened, and ask nothing of you. That's the relationship we wanted with our money.

We read ours every morning. Then we started handing it to friends. It turns out the quiet relief of simply knowing isn't a niche feeling at all.

Who it's for

Some of the people who tend to like it:

The Mint refugee

Mint died, and everything that replaced it wants $15 a month, your bank login, or both. This is the boring middle — it just shows you your spending.

The privacy-minded

If handing a "trusted" aggregator your bank credentials is a hard no, bank alerts let you track spending without ever sharing a password.

The couple who wants one baseline

A morning email you both read replaces "what was this charge at Target?" — because you both already know. No surveillance, no being kept in the dark.

The subscription forgetter

The forgotten free trials and quiet price hikes — surfaced and totaled, including the ones you'd never think to go looking for.

The almost-saver

You earn enough to save and somehow don't. Seeing where it actually goes is the part no app ever put in front of you.

The freelancer dreading April

Tag a charge as a work expense, attach the receipt, and export the whole bundle at tax time. It replaces the shoebox.

The lapsed spreadsheet keeper

The spreadsheet was never the problem — the daily data entry was. Forwarding happens on its own, so tracking finally sticks.

The one in transition

New baby, new house, new job, a layoff. When the old autopilot doesn't fit, a daily look helps you learn the new shape of things.

The over-alerted

Fifty bank alerts a day you've started ignoring? Same information, one calm briefing every morning instead of fifty pings.

However you got here, the promise is the same: one calm email every morning, and you'll know.
